Modern Chess

While the entire history of chess dates back to the 7th century CE, the history of "modern" chess begins in 1861, when a time limit for making moves was introduced. The time limit was introduced to make matches more fair and competitive as people used to take several hours calculating and planning moves, taking up too much time.
Starting with sandglasses, the items used for players to track time evolved gradually, from sandglasses to pendulums, then into the familiar modern clocks which have parallel timers and a button to press after each move.

American Paul Morphy, an extraordinary chess prodigy, won against all important competitors, including Anderssen, during his short chess career between 1857 and 1863.
Through his success and innovative way of playing chess, he pioneers an aggressive style of chess that was daring and reckless, using a combination of impressive attacks and sound strategy.
Wilheim Steinitz becomes the first official world chess champion in World Chess Championship (that is still running today) history.
His biggest contribution to chess was the advance of the game's strategic understanding with clever use of bishops and knights to control the board.
Aron Nimzowitsch and Richard Réti revolutionizes chess by advocating for control of the center board using distant pieces like bishops rather than pawns, instead inviting the usage of pawns to attack the opponent's formation. This introduces a layer of depth for players to consider during games, making it more complex.
Following the world war's end, an era of Soviet domination is ushered by Russian Mikhail Botvinnik, where the only exception was an American, Bobby Fischer's period in 1972-1975.
Despite breaking the mold and having a chance to overturn this era of Soviet dominance, he refused to defend his title going forward. Within this period, there were many changes made to qualifications for the World Chess Championship.
Soon after his first win and the beginning of his time at the top, Garry Kasparov launches ChessBase, the first ever computer database program, in collaboration with Frederic Friedel.
In 1996, Kasparov played a pair of six-game exhibition matches against IBM supercomputer Deep Blue. He won the first match 4-2 but lost the rematch 3½-2½(draws are counted as half). This defeat symbolically heralded the arrival of chess engines playing at grandmaster strength.
Magnus Carlsen wins his first championship in 2013, defending it 4 more times until he refused to defend it in 2023. Within this period, chess gets a boost in popularity through online platforms where people can play and match against others worldwide for free.
The most popular websites feature quick pairing systems and site-specific ratings for bullet, blitz, rapid, and/or classical time controls.

Origin of chess

When it comes to the earliest roots of chess, its history goes way back to 7th century CE. At this point, chess looked rather different, with its main resemblance being in the core idea of using 4 unique pieces to strategically defeat the opponent.

Originating in India, an early form of chess was developped in 7th century CE. Known as Chaturaṅga, it translates to "four divisions", representing infantry, cavalry, elephantry, chariotry. Later, these pieces would evolve into the modern versions being the pawn, knight, bishop and rook respectively.
Chess gets introduced to Persia in around 600 CE. Due to the differences in language, some name pronounciations could not be replicated in Persia. This led to developments like players calling "Shāh!" when attacking the king, and "Shāh! Māt!" when the king was attacked and had no way to escape.
After the Arab Muslims conquered the Persian Empire, chess gets spread to various muslim countries. Only a few changes were made to the game during this period, and they were mostly just changes in names or terms to fit the local language.
Chess eventually spreads to Europe, then to Russia, and subsequently to other countries in the world. Chess in Europe is developped with some rule changes, as well as the inroduction of new ones. Other countries instead decide to add their own spin, evolving the game into their own unique versions like Xiangqi in China and Shogi in Japan.

Famous Players

Despite the numerous chess champions throughout the years, few have stood out and really made a name for themselves. Be it through dominance or revolutionizing and advancing the game, these players stood out as the best of the best, considered by even grandmasters today as the greatest of all time.

Bobby Fischer

Bobby Fischer became a great player from a young age, being the youngest to win the U.S Chess Championship in 1957/1958. He proceeds to win the next 8 U.S Chess Championships that he participates in, and achieves a perfect win in the 1963/1964 championship.
Later on, he accomplishes an all time record of 20 consecutive wins in World Chess Championship qualifying events from 1970-1971 before going on to win the championship in 1972. This win was an outlier, with the past champions in recent years being all Soviets and having the odds stacked against him.
American chess players had less support compared to Soviets, with the latter receiving state support. In addition, America had far less players at 5000, compared to the 5000000 of the Soviets, making his training even harder.
Despite his strength, Fischer refused to defend his title in 1975 and stepped away from the chess scene till 1992 due to disagreements with FIDE, who is the governing body for the World Chess Championship.

Garry Kasparov

Garry Kasparov was recognized as a prodigy from a young age, winning both top-level junior & adult events, including 2 first-place ties at the USSR Championship as a teen.
He went on to defeat Karpov, the reigning chess champion at the time, at age 22 to become the youngest word chess champion ever.
Later, Kasparov would break away from FIDE's jurisdiction, forming his own Professional Chess Association. He, like Bobby Fischer, disagreed with FIDE's management and additionally suspected there was corruption within the organisation.
During this split, he would aid in the development of chess engines, as well as play 2 of the most iconic and well-publicized chess engine face offs. Currently, chess engines are widely used for players of all skill levels to practice.
He would also later go on to coach Magnus Carlsen, the greatest chess player of today, for a year in 2009.

Magnus Carlsen

Magnus CarlSen is known as one of the most dominant players in chess history, as is evident by his sheer number of accolades. One prime example of his skill being the youngest chess grandmaster in the world at 13 years old in 2010. The title of grandmaster is something only achieved by players in their late teens or early twenties.
Currently, he is a five-time world chess champion (until he refused to defend his title in 2023), five-time world rapid chess champion and reigning world blitz chess champion.
He surpassed the FIDE rating of 2800 at age 18, the youngest at the time to do so, then going on to reach No.1 in the FIDE world ranking at age 19, the youngest ever to do so.
Despite stepping away from the championship in 2023, people never doubted his abilities as the best, some even joking that he stepped away because he was bored of winning, which is half true as he claimed to have no motivation.
